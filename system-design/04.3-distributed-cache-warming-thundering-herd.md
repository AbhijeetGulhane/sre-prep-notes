# Distributed Cache — Warming & Deploy-Time Thundering Herd

*Companion to [04-distributed-cache-nalsd.md](./04-distributed-cache-nalsd.md) and [04-distributed-cache-eviction-stampede.md](./04-distributed-cache-eviction-stampede.md). Deliberately a separate doc from the stampede one — this is a different failure shape, worth not conflating with it.*

**The distinction that matters:** the earlier stampede doc covered *one hot key* expiring with many concurrent readers — a narrow, single-key event a `SETNX` lock fully solves. This doc covers the cache being **cold across a wide swath of the keyspace at once** — a new deploy, a cluster restart, a node added during a rescale — where near-*every* read misses simultaneously, not just one key's readers. A per-key lock still helps, but it doesn't touch the actual problem here, which is the sheer *number* of distinct keys hitting the DB at once, not duplicate queries for the same one.

---

## 1. Why this happens

- **A full cache cluster restart or `FLUSHALL`** — every key gone at once, worst case.
- **A rolling deploy replacing nodes one at a time** — each individual node swap is a *localized* cold-start for whatever slice of the keyspace that one node owned, smaller blast radius than a full-cluster event, but still a real one.
- **Scaling the cluster up** — a newly added node, per the consistent-hashing ring (doc 03, Section 3), immediately owns some fraction of the keyspace via its assigned vnodes, but starts with **zero data**. Every read that hashes to it is a guaranteed miss until it's been populated, purely as a side effect of the rebalance — not because the data was ever actually cold, just because it moved.

## 2. Quantifying why this is a bigger danger than a single-key stampede

Doc 04's own capacity numbers make this concrete. The backing DB was sized around a **95% cache hit ratio** — only the resulting 5% miss traffic, 50,000 ops/sec, was ever expected to reach it (doc 04, Section 1).

**A cold cache doesn't produce 5% miss traffic — it produces close to 100%:**
```
Normal steady state:  1,000,000 req/sec × (1 − 0.95 hit ratio) =    50,000 req/sec to DB
Cold-cache event:      1,000,000 req/sec × (1 − ~0.00 hit ratio) ≈ 1,000,000 req/sec to DB

Overload factor: 1,000,000 ÷ 50,000 = 20x the DB's provisioned load
```
**A cold cache can throw roughly 20x the DB's sustainable capacity at it — from a deploy event, with no increase in actual user traffic at all.** This is categorically worse than the single-key stampede in the companion doc, which concentrated load on one key's DB query; this concentrates near-*all* application traffic onto the DB simultaneously, because the layer whose entire job is preventing that (the cache) is the thing that just went cold.

---

## 3. Cache warming — the primary fix: never expose the near-0% hit-ratio state to live traffic at all

### Approach A: Snapshot restore before joining the ring
If the cache layer supports persistence (an RDB-style periodic snapshot, mentioned as optional in doc 04's follow-ups), load the most recent snapshot into a new or replaced node **before** it's added to the consistent-hashing ring and starts receiving live reads. The data is slightly stale (as of the snapshot time) but already present — the node never serves a true cold miss for anything that existed at snapshot time.

### Approach B: Proactive top-N warm load
Real-world access patterns are heavily skewed — the same Pareto-style concentration doc 03's hot-key discussion assumed. Identify the most-accessed keys from recent access logs/analytics and explicitly pre-fetch just that hot subset into the new node before cutover, rather than attempting to warm the entire keyspace:
```
If the top 1% of keys account for ~80% of traffic (a realistic, commonly observed
concentration for this kind of access pattern), warming just that 1% before cutover
absorbs the large majority of what would otherwise be cold-miss traffic —
at a small fraction of the cost of warming everything.
```
This is far cheaper than a full snapshot restore when only a subset of keys actually matters for hit-ratio recovery, and it's the right default when there's no recent snapshot available at all.

### Approach C: Shadow traffic replay
Mirror a fraction of live production read traffic to the new/cold node **before** it's actually serving real responses — a sidecar or service-mesh layer duplicates requests to the new node in "shadow" mode, letting it warm up via genuine access patterns without any live request depending on its (currently cold) answers. Once its observed hit rate on shadow traffic reaches an acceptable level, it can be safely cut into the real serving path.

### Approach D: Gradual traffic ramp via weighted ring entry
Rather than a new node taking on its full share of the keyspace instantly, assign it a **small number of vnodes initially** (doc 03, Section 3's vnode mechanism), then increase its vnode count gradually over minutes-to-hours. This spreads the node's cold-start miss burden over a longer window instead of concentrating it at one cutover instant — the same underlying idea as the eviction-stampede doc's jittered TTLs (spread load out over time instead of synchronizing it), applied here to a node's entry into the cluster rather than a key's expiration.

---

## 4. Rolling deploys: keep the blast radius small by construction

The single most effective structural mitigation: **never replace or flush the whole cluster at once.** Roll node-by-node (or shard-by-shard), applying whichever warming approach above to each node individually before it takes live traffic, and moving to the next node only once the previous one has recovered a healthy hit ratio. At any given moment, only a small fraction (~1/N, where N is the cluster size) of the keyspace is cold — not the entire cache — which directly bounds how much of the Section 2 overload factor can actually materialize at once, regardless of which warming technique is used underneath.

---

## 5. Defense-in-depth for whatever residual herd still gets through

Warming reduces the problem; it rarely eliminates every miss during a transition. Layer these underneath as insurance:

- **Per-key request coalescing / `SETNX` locks** (from the companion stampede doc) still help — they don't reduce the *number* of distinct keys missing at once, but they do prevent each of those misses from being independently re-queried by every concurrent reader of that specific key, which is real load reduction on top of whatever warming already achieved.
- **Rate-limit the DB specifically for cache-miss-triggered queries** — this is a direct, deliberate reuse of doc 02's rate limiter design, applied as a protective layer in front of the DB rather than in front of a public API. A cold cache then degrades to "some requests queued or briefly slowed" instead of "DB falls over entirely" — the rate limiter's whole purpose (protect a downstream system from more load than it can handle) applies exactly as well to a cache-induced internal overload as to external API abuse.
- **Circuit breaker on DB calls** — if DB error rate or latency spikes past a threshold during a herd event, trip the breaker and fail fast (or serve degraded results) rather than letting a growing queue of retries compound the overload. This prevents the classic retry-storm failure mode, where clients timing out and retrying *adds* load to an already-overloaded DB instead of relieving it.
- **Serve stale-if-available as an emergency valve** — if any prior value for a key is still reachable (a not-yet-evicted old cache entry, a recent snapshot value), serve it with a "stale" marker rather than blocking every request on a fresh DB fetch during the overload window. This is the same availability-over-strict-correctness trade doc 03's AP design made deliberately (Section 1 of that doc) — applied here as a temporary, explicitly-bounded escape valve during a known transition event, not a permanent posture.

---

## 6. Combined procedure for an actual production deploy

1. **Never flush or replace the whole cluster simultaneously** — roll node-by-node.
2. **Warm each node before it joins the ring**: snapshot restore if available, otherwise proactive top-N load from recent access logs.
3. **Ramp traffic in gradually** via weighted vnode assignment rather than an instant full cutover.
4. **Keep the DB-side rate limiter and circuit breaker running underneath regardless** — insurance for whatever miss burst gets through despite steps 1–3, not a substitute for them.

---

## 7. Follow-up questions

- **"How do you decide when a warmed node is ready for full traffic?"** — monitor its actual observed hit ratio (or shadow-traffic hit ratio, Approach C) against a threshold close to the cluster's steady-state target (95% in doc 04's numbers) before increasing its vnode share further — a data-driven ramp, not a fixed timer.
- **"What if the whole DB itself needs to be provisioned to survive a cold-cache worst case, not just rate-limited around it?"** — that's a legitimate, more expensive alternative: size the DB for some fraction of the Section 2 overload factor rather than assuming warming and rate-limiting will always fully prevent it — a cost/risk trade-off worth naming explicitly rather than assuming warming alone is sufficient insurance for a system where a DB outage is unacceptable.
- **"Does this connect to the URL shortener or KV store docs at all?"** — yes, directly: doc 01's CDN-edge warming problem (a brand-new deploy or region has cold edges too) and doc 03's rebalance-on-resize (Section 3's vnode remapping) are the same underlying pattern — a topology change creates a temporarily cold subset of a system that was previously warm, and the fix in every case is controlling the blast radius and ramping traffic gradually rather than cutting over instantly.
