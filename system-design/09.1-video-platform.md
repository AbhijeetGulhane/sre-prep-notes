## 1. Requirements & Capacity Estimation

### Functional requirements
- **Upload** raw video, durably, resilient to network interruption on large files.
- **Transcode** into multiple resolution/bitrate renditions for adaptive playback.
- **Generate thumbnails.**
- **Store and serve metadata** — title, description, channel, processing status, view counts.
- **Play back** video globally with fast startup and smooth quality adaptation to network conditions.

### Non-functional requirements
- **Upload must never block on transcoding.** A multi-hour source video can take a genuinely long time to fully process — this single fact shapes the entire pipeline (Section 3).
- **Playback read volume dwarfs upload volume by orders of magnitude** — a single popular video is watched vastly more times than it was ever uploaded, the same read-skew shape as every prior doc, just more extreme here.
- **Playback startup latency and mid-playback quality adaptation matter as much as raw throughput** — this is a product-quality requirement, not just a capacity one (Section 7).
- **View counts don't need to be exactly accurate in real time** — an important, deliberate scope-narrowing that Section 5 leans on directly.

### Scale target
- **500 hours of video uploaded per minute** (a commonly cited industry-scale figure, used here as a capacity-planning assumption, the same way this series has set round-number scale targets throughout — not a claim about any specific platform's current real numbers).
- **Average source bitrate: 5 Mbps** (a blended figure across the full mix of resolutions being uploaded).

```
Video-time arriving per minute: 500 hours × 60 min/hour = 30,000 minutes of footage per 1 minute of wall clock
                              → 30,000x real-time, aggregate

Aggregate ingestion bandwidth: 30,000 × 5 Mbps = 150,000 Mbps = 150 Gbps ≈ 18.75 GB/sec

Daily raw footage volume: 500 hours/min × 60 min/hour × 24 hour/day = 720,000 hours/day
Daily raw storage growth: 720,000 hours × 2.25 GB/hour (at 5 Mbps) ≈ 1.62 PB/day (raw source only)
```

## 2. High-Level Architecture

```
Client ──resumable upload──▶ Upload service ──▶ Raw video → GCS (object storage)
                                    │
                              Enqueue transcode job (durable queue, partitioned by video_id)
                                    │
                        ┌───────────┼───────────┐
                  Transcode workers (FFmpeg)  Thumbnail extraction
                        │                           │
                  Renditions → GCS            Thumbnail images → GCS
                        │
                  Metadata update (Spanner): status → "ready", rendition manifest recorded
                        │
                  CDN pulls renditions to edge on first playback request
                        │
                  Client player: fetch manifest → adaptive bitrate segment requests
```

## 3. Upload → Transcode: The Same Accept-Fast, Process-Async Pattern as Doc 07

**This is a direct reuse of doc 07's MTA design, not a coincidence** — the underlying shape is identical: the upload service's only job is to **durably persist the raw upload** (to GCS) and **enqueue a transcode job**, then respond to the client immediately with a "processing" status. It does not wait for transcoding to complete, for exactly the reason doc 07's MTA didn't wait for spam filtering and indexing to complete before acknowledging an SMTP sender: the downstream work can take a genuinely long time (minutes to hours for a long source video), and blocking the accept path on it would make upload reliability depend on transcoding reliability, which is both unnecessary and fragile.

**Queue partitioning**: by `video_id`, the same "partition by owning entity" principle that's shown up for every queue in this series (doc 07's recipient-partitioned MTA queue, doc 06's host-partitioned crawler frontier) — keeps all processing steps for one video ordered and independent of every other video's processing.

**Resumable upload, worth naming explicitly**: large video files are exactly the case where a naive single-request upload is fragile — a network interruption partway through a multi-GB upload shouldn't mean starting over. A chunked/resumable upload protocol (uploading in smaller pieces with the ability to resume from the last successfully received chunk) is a real, necessary detail for this system's durability story, not an implementation afterthought.

## 4. Transcoding — FFmpeg, Adaptive Bitrate, and Parallelizing a Single Job

### Why multiple renditions at all — adaptive bitrate streaming (ABR)
A single fixed-quality video is a bad experience across the full range of real-world network conditions: too high a bitrate stutters on a weak connection, too low a bitrate wastes quality on a strong one. The fix: encode the source into **several renditions** (e.g., 240p/360p/480p/720p/1080p, each at an appropriate bitrate), split each rendition into short segments (a few seconds each — the HLS/DASH standard approach), and generate a **manifest** listing every available rendition and its segment URLs. **The client player**, not the server, continuously measures its own achieved throughput and requests whichever rendition's segments currently fit — switching seamlessly mid-playback as conditions change (a WiFi-to-cellular handoff, for instance). This is the actual mechanism behind visible quality changes during playback, not a server-side decision imposed on the client.

### Why FFmpeg specifically
An industry-standard, open-source transcoding engine supporting essentially every codec/container combination in wide use, scriptable and automatable as a CLI/library inside a distributed worker fleet — this is a tool-choice, not a novel design decision, and worth naming explicitly since it's the de facto default rather than one option among several genuinely competing ones.

### Parallelizing a single video's transcode job
A long source video transcoded as one serial job — first render rendition 1 fully, then rendition 2, and so on — would take a long wall-clock time regardless of how much aggregate cluster capacity exists elsewhere. Two levels of parallelism fix this:
- **Across renditions**: each target rendition is an independent job, run in parallel rather than sequentially.
- **Within a rendition**: the source can be split into time-segments, each transcoded independently and in parallel across multiple workers, then concatenated — a MapReduce-shaped decomposition of a single video's own processing job.

**This matters for a reason worked out precisely in Section 7**: it doesn't reduce the *total* compute cost of transcoding a video — it changes how that fixed cost is distributed *across time*, which is a latency fix, not a throughput fix. Worth keeping that distinction sharp; it's easy to conflate the two.

## 5. Metadata — Spanner, and the CP Answer Doc 03.1 Left Open

### Object storage vs. a real database, again
Video bytes and thumbnails belong in **GCS** (object storage) — the same "large blobs don't belong in a row/key-value store sized for small values" lesson doc 06 established for crawled content and doc 07 established for email attachments, now recurring a fourth time. Metadata (title, description, channel, processing status, ownership) belongs in a proper queryable database.

### Why Spanner, and what it actually answers
Doc 03.1's KV store made a deliberate, explicit **AP** choice — availability over consistency, quorum reads/writes, vector clocks for conflict resolution — and its own follow-up questions section explicitly flagged "what if you need strong (linearizable) consistency for some keys?" as a real gap, naming CP systems only in the abstract ("Raft/Paxos, à la Zookeeper/etcd or Spanner's TrueTime-backed design") without developing one. **This is that system, named and explained.**

**Spanner is Google's globally-distributed, strongly-consistent database** — it achieves CP-style external consistency *at global scale* through **TrueTime**, a globally-synchronized clock API (backed by GPS and atomic clocks) that lets Spanner assign globally meaningful commit timestamps with a bounded, known uncertainty window, combined with Paxos-based replication per data shard. This is a genuinely different mechanism from doc 03.1's approach — not quorum-plus-vector-clocks accepting occasional siblings, but synchronized-clocks-plus-consensus giving a single, globally ordered, unambiguous answer to "what happened when." Worth stating precisely: this isn't "Spanner cheats the CAP theorem" — it's a real, substantial engineering investment (globally synchronized, bounded-uncertainty clocks) that buys strong consistency at a scale and availability level that would otherwise require choosing AP instead.

**Why metadata specifically benefits from this**: video ownership, monetization status, and processing state changes (upload → processing → ready/failed) are exactly the kind of data where doc 03.1's vector-clock siblings-and-merge model would be the wrong fit — there's no meaningful "merge" of two concurrent claims about who owns a video, unlike doc 03.1's shopping-cart example where merging genuinely made sense. Relational query capability (find all videos by this channel, join channels to playlists) is also a real, direct benefit a pure KV store doesn't offer cleanly.

### The honest exception: view counts
**Not everything in the metadata layer should get Spanner's strong-consistency treatment**, and view counts are the clearest example. Routing every single view increment through Spanner's consensus path would be an unnecessary write-serialization bottleneck for a number the product explicitly doesn't need to be exactly accurate in real time (Section 1). View counts are far better served by an **approximate, eventually-consistent counting pipeline** — conceptually closer to doc 02's rate-limiter-style counters or doc 04's cache-based aggregation than to a Spanner-backed row, periodically reconciled rather than synchronously consistent on every increment. Worth stating this as a deliberate per-data-type decision, not a blanket "everything goes in Spanner" default — the same instinct as doc 03.2's "does every key need vector clocks, or can you choose per key-type?" follow-up question, just answered concretely here instead of left open.

## 6. Thumbnails

A comparatively simple sub-problem, worth keeping proportionate rather than over-engineering:
- **Extract candidate frames** at set intervals during the same decode pass already happening for transcoding — reusing that pass rather than a separate full re-decode is a real efficiency win worth naming, not just an implementation detail.
- **Selection**: either simple fixed timestamps (e.g., 25%/50%/75% through the video) or basic heuristics to avoid black frames/scene transitions — this doesn't need to be sophisticated to meet the requirement.
- Store thumbnails in GCS alongside renditions; let the uploader override with a custom thumbnail or pick among generated candidates.

## 7. CDN and Playback — Where Every Prior Doc's Caching Lesson Converges

**Playback traffic dominates this system's read path by a wide margin** — the same read-skew argument from doc 01 (shortener redirects) and doc 04 (distributed cache), just more extreme, since a single popular video's lifetime view count can be enormous relative to its single upload event. **CDN edge caching absorbs the overwhelming majority of playback bandwidth**, with GCS (the origin) only ever seeing cache-miss traffic — precisely doc 04's cache-aside hit-ratio argument, applied to video segments instead of key-value pairs, and precisely doc 01's CDN-for-hot-links argument, applied to video instead of redirects. Worth being explicit that this system is where those two previously-separate lessons turn out to be the same lesson.

**Startup latency ("time to first frame")**: the client fetches the manifest first, then deliberately requests a **low, safe-bitrate segment initially** to start playback quickly, ramping up to a higher rendition only once it has measured real throughput — the opposite of naively requesting the "ideal" bitrate first, which would add avoidable startup delay in exchange for marginally better initial quality nobody benefits from if the video hasn't started playing yet.

## 8. Capacity Math and the First Real Bottleneck

### Upload ingestion — bandwidth-bound, the same shape as doc 07's finding
```
18.75 GB/sec aggregate ingestion bandwidth (Section 1)
÷ 1.25 GB/sec/node (a 10Gbps NIC, same reference class as doc 07)
= 15 nodes
```
**This is the third time in this series bandwidth, not request count, has turned out to be the real ingestion constraint** (doc 07's email MTA was the second) — worth having that pattern fully internalized by now: any system moving large payloads needs its first-pass capacity math to check bytes/sec, not just requests/sec, as a matter of habit.

### Transcoding compute — the actual dominant cost
```
Assume transcoding 1 hour of source video into all target renditions
takes ~0.5 hours of compute-equivalent worker-time (illustrative, real
figures vary widely by codec/resolution/hardware acceleration)

720,000 hours/day of raw footage × 0.5 compute-hour/hour of footage
= 360,000 compute-hours/day of transcoding work needed

360,000 ÷ 24 hours = 15,000 continuously-running transcode workers
                      needed at steady state
```
**15,000 workers dwarfs the 15-node ingestion layer by three orders of magnitude.** This is the same shape of finding as doc 07's search-indexing layer turning out to be the real cost center, not the SMTP front door — here, **transcoding, not upload ingestion, is overwhelmingly the expensive part of this system**, and it's worth having that specific, quantified contrast ready rather than the vague intuition that "video processing is probably expensive."

### The first real bottleneck, and a genuinely different kind of "iterate" than usual
Given transcoding so clearly dominates total resource cost, the natural instinct is to look for a fix that reduces the 15,000-worker number. **There isn't one that doesn't also reduce quality or rendition count** — the total compute-hours needed is close to a fixed function of how much footage arrives and how many renditions are generated; it's conserved work, not a provisioning mistake to correct.

**What Section 4's parallelization *does* fix is a different problem entirely: latency for any one video, not aggregate capacity.** Splitting a single long video's transcode across many parallel worker-segments doesn't change the total compute-hours consumed — it changes how that same fixed cost is distributed **across time** for that one job. A 3-hour source video transcoded serially takes a long single-job wall time regardless of total fleet size; the same video split across 12 parallel segment-workers finishes roughly 12x faster in wall-clock terms, at the same aggregate compute cost. **Worth stating this distinction explicitly and precisely**: this is a bottleneck with a real fix, but the fix is a *latency* lever, not a *capacity* lever — a different shape from doc 06's genuinely unfixable politeness ceiling, and different again from doc 03.3's N=3→N=5 trade (which *did* reduce a failure rate at a real capacity cost). Three different "iterate" shapes across three different docs, worth having all three distinguished clearly: some bottlenecks have no fix at all (doc 06), some trade capacity for a better outcome (doc 03.3), and some — this one — trade nothing in aggregate, only redistributing existing cost across time instead.

## 11. Follow-up Questions

- **"How do you handle a transcode job failure partway through?"** — the same durable-queue guarantee as doc 07: a failed worker's job is retried from the queue, not lost, since the raw source in GCS and the job record both persisted before processing began — retry safety was designed in from Section 3, not bolted on after.
- **"Why not transcode on upload synchronously for short videos, since they'd finish fast anyway?"** — tempting, but it reintroduces exactly the coupling Section 3 avoided: a system with two different reliability models (synchronous for short videos, async for long ones) is a real operational complexity cost for a latency win that a well-designed async path with a fast "processing → ready" transition can mostly capture anyway.
- **"How does this connect to a future recommendation/search system?"** — deliberately out of scope here (Section 1), but worth naming as the natural next layer: recommendations and search both consume the metadata this doc produces (Spanner records, view-count signals) as their own input, the same way doc 06's crawler output would feed a search-ranking system this series hasn't built yet.
